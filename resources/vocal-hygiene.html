Our goal is not to change your diet or take away things that bring you joy. We are just giving you this information so you can make informed decisions about factors that affect your vocal hygiene and ease of use.

Diet

  • Hydrate, hydrate, hydrate! Room temperature or warm water (or hot herbal tea) are ideal in the morning or before practicing to increase blood flow. Ice water can be helpful in reducing inflammation, so that's a good option at the end of the day if your voice is tired. Try not to speak for 30 minutes after drinking ice water.
  • Alcohol is dehydrating, so drink extra water if you are drinking alcohol.
  • Dairy and citrus can create phlegm for some folks. If you notice that you have to clear your throat often after eating dairy or citrus, consider limiting your intake the day of your voice lesson.
  • Caffeine is a diuretic, so drink water alongside your caffeinated beverages.

Lifestyle

  • Rest when your voice is tired or you have a sore throat. We'd rather you rest and recover than try to push and practice through illness.
  • Sleep is crucial! When your body is tired, your voice will be tired and harder to use.
  • Smoking (nicotine or marijuana) is extremely dehydrating and can cause tongue tension.
  • Stress and anxiety make it harder to breathe deeply, so spend extra time on breath and relaxation exercises on tough mental health days.
  • If you grind your teeth, consider speaking with your dentist about a night guard.
  • Consider steam inhalations to hydrate your vocal folds if you have postnasal drip, seasonal allergies, or a cold, or if you take spironolactone or other dehydrating medications. You can buy a personal steamer, or boil a pot of water and lean over it with a towel over your head. Careful not to burn yourself!
  • Consider using a cool mist humidifier if you live in a dry climate.
  • Try to limit throat clearing. Instead, try humming on a low pitch and then drinking some water.
  • If you have acid reflux, try not to practice right after eating. The abdominal pressure from voice work can exacerbate reflux symptoms.
